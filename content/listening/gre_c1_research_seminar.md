A: Your paper on cognitive load theory was fascinating. Do you think the dual-channel model adequately accounts for individual differences in working memory capacity?
B: That's a perceptive question. The dual-channel model, as Mayer originally proposed it, treats working memory capacity as relatively uniform. But empirical evidence increasingly suggests substantial individual variation that the model doesn't fully capture.
A: So would you argue for a more dynamic model that incorporates individual differences as a variable rather than a constant?
B: Precisely. What I'm proposing is a probabilistic framework where cognitive load thresholds are modeled as distributions rather than fixed values. This would allow us to predict learning outcomes with greater precision across diverse populations.
A: How does this interact with your findings on multimedia learning? You mentioned that visual-verbal redundancy sometimes enhances rather than impairs learning.
B: Right, and that's where it gets interesting. The redundancy effect appears to be moderated by prior knowledge. For novices, redundancy creates extraneous load. But for experts, the same redundancy can serve as a retrieval cue, actually reducing effective cognitive load.
A: That has significant implications for instructional design. Are you planning to test this with neuroimaging data?
B: We're currently designing an fMRI study to examine neural correlates of cognitive load under varying redundancy conditions. We expect to have preliminary results by next spring.
