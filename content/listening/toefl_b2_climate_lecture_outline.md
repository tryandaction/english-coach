A: Today I'd like to explain the urban heat island effect. First, I'll define the term and describe why cities are usually warmer than nearby rural areas.
A: After that, I'll look at three main causes: dark surfaces such as asphalt that absorb heat, dense building materials that release heat slowly at night, and reduced vegetation, which limits natural cooling through shade and evaporation.
A: Then I'll move to the consequences. Higher nighttime temperatures increase energy demand, worsen air quality, and raise health risks during heat waves, especially for older residents.
A: Finally, I'll discuss responses that cities can use, including reflective roofs, more tree cover, and redesigned public spaces that improve airflow.
A: Keep this structure in mind because the solutions only make sense after we understand both the causes and the effects.
