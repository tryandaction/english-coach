A: One of the most enduring puzzles in the philosophy of language concerns the relationship between meaning and reference. When we use a proper name like "Aristotle," what exactly are we referring to, and how does that name acquire its meaning?
A: The descriptivist view, associated with Frege and Russell, holds that names are shorthand for definite descriptions. So "Aristotle" might mean "the student of Plato who taught Alexander the Great." The name refers to whoever satisfies that description.
A: Kripke challenged this view decisively in his 1970 lectures, later published as Naming and Necessity. He argued that names are rigid designators — they refer to the same individual in all possible worlds. Even if Aristotle had never taught Alexander, we would still be talking about Aristotle, not someone else.
A: Kripke introduced the causal-historical theory of reference. A name is introduced in an initial baptism — someone points to an individual and assigns a name. That name then passes through a chain of communication, with each speaker intending to use it to refer to the same individual as the person they learned it from.
A: This has profound implications. It means that the meaning of a name is not in our heads — it's partly constituted by the external world and the historical chain of reference. This is what Putnam called semantic externalism: meaning just ain't in the head.
