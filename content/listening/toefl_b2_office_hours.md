A: Professor Davis, do you have a few minutes? I had some questions about the assignment.
B: Of course, come in. What's on your mind?
A: I'm not sure I understand the difference between correlation and causation in the context of our data analysis project.
B: That's a really important distinction. Correlation means two variables move together, but it doesn't mean one causes the other. For example, ice cream sales and drowning rates both increase in summer, but ice cream doesn't cause drowning.
A: Oh, I see. So for my project on study hours and grades, even if they correlate, I can't say studying causes better grades?
B: Exactly. There could be confounding variables — like student motivation or prior knowledge — that explain both. To establish causation, you'd need a controlled experiment.
A: That makes sense. Should I mention this limitation in my paper?
B: Absolutely. Acknowledging limitations shows critical thinking. It will strengthen your analysis, not weaken it.
A: Thank you. One more thing — is it acceptable to use secondary data sources?
B: Yes, as long as you cite them properly and evaluate their reliability. Check the methodology section of any study you use.
