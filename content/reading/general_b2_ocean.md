The ocean covers more than 70 percent of Earth's surface and contains the majority of the planet's living space. Ocean ecosystems support an extraordinary diversity of life, from microscopic phytoplankton to the largest animals on Earth. These ecosystems provide essential services to humanity, including food, climate regulation, and the production of oxygen — roughly half of the oxygen we breathe is generated by marine phytoplankton.

Coral reefs are among the most biologically diverse ecosystems on the planet. Although they cover less than one percent of the ocean floor, they are home to approximately 25 percent of all marine species. Corals are tiny animals that build calcium carbonate skeletons, forming the structural foundation of the reef. They live in a symbiotic relationship with photosynthetic algae called zooxanthellae, which provide the coral with nutrients through photosynthesis.

Ocean ecosystems face serious threats from human activity. Rising water temperatures caused by climate change lead to coral bleaching, a process in which corals expel their symbiotic algae and turn white. If temperatures remain elevated, the corals die. Overfishing has depleted populations of many commercially important species, disrupting food webs. Plastic pollution is another growing problem — millions of tonnes of plastic enter the ocean each year, harming marine animals that ingest or become entangled in it.

Conservation efforts include the establishment of marine protected areas, where fishing and other extractive activities are restricted. Scientists are also researching ways to help corals adapt to warmer temperatures. Protecting ocean ecosystems is not only important for biodiversity but also for the billions of people who depend on the sea for food and livelihoods.
