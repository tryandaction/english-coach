Microplastics — plastic particles smaller than five millimeters — have become one of the most pervasive environmental contaminants of the modern era. Since the term was coined in 2004 by marine biologist Richard Thompson, researchers have detected microplastics in virtually every ecosystem on Earth, from the deepest ocean trenches to the peaks of remote mountain ranges.

The primary sources of microplastics are the fragmentation of larger plastic debris through UV radiation and mechanical abrasion, as well as the direct release of microbeads from personal care products and synthetic fibers shed during laundry. A single wash cycle of a polyester garment can release hundreds of thousands of microfibers into wastewater systems, many of which pass through conventional filtration.

The ecological consequences are significant. Marine organisms from zooplankton to whales ingest microplastics, which can cause physical blockages, reduce feeding behavior, and introduce toxic chemical additives into the food chain. Studies have shown that microplastics can cross biological membranes and accumulate in tissues, raising concerns about bioaccumulation across trophic levels.

Human exposure is now considered unavoidable. Microplastics have been found in drinking water, sea salt, honey, beer, and even human blood and lung tissue. The health implications remain under investigation, but preliminary studies suggest potential inflammatory responses and endocrine disruption from associated chemical additives such as phthalates and bisphenol A.

Addressing microplastic pollution requires action at multiple levels: redesigning products to minimize plastic use, improving wastewater treatment infrastructure, and developing international agreements to regulate plastic production and disposal. Without systemic change, projections suggest that plastic production will triple by 2060, dramatically worsening the problem.
