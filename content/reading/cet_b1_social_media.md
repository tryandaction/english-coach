Social media platforms have transformed the way people communicate, share information, and form relationships. With over five billion users worldwide, platforms like WeChat, Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter have become central to daily life, particularly for young people. However, growing evidence suggests that heavy social media use is associated with negative mental health outcomes.

Research consistently links excessive social media use to increased rates of anxiety, depression, and loneliness, especially among teenagers. One mechanism is social comparison — users constantly see carefully curated highlights of others' lives, leading to feelings of inadequacy. Studies show that passive scrolling, where users consume content without interacting, is more harmful than active engagement such as messaging friends.

Sleep disruption is another significant concern. The blue light emitted by screens suppresses melatonin production, making it harder to fall asleep. Many young people check their phones immediately before bed and first thing in the morning, disrupting natural sleep cycles. Poor sleep, in turn, worsens mood and cognitive performance.

However, social media is not uniformly harmful. For people who are isolated — due to disability, geography, or social anxiety — online communities can provide genuine connection and support. During the COVID-19 pandemic, social media helped many people maintain relationships and access mental health resources.

The key appears to be intentional use. Setting time limits, curating feeds to include positive and educational content, and prioritizing face-to-face interaction can mitigate the negative effects. Several countries are now considering legislation to restrict social media access for children under sixteen, reflecting growing concern about the impact on developing minds.
