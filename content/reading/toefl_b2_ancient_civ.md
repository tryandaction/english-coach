The ancient city of Mesopotamia, located between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers in modern-day Iraq, is widely regarded as one of the earliest centers of human civilization. Around 3500 BCE, the Sumerians developed one of the world's first writing systems, known as cuneiform, which used wedge-shaped marks pressed into clay tablets. This innovation allowed for the recording of trade transactions, laws, and literary works, fundamentally transforming how societies organized and preserved knowledge.

Mesopotamian civilization also made significant advances in mathematics and astronomy. Scholars developed a base-60 number system, which still influences how we measure time and angles today. They carefully observed the movements of celestial bodies and created calendars to predict seasonal changes, which was essential for agricultural planning.

The city-states of Mesopotamia were governed by kings who claimed divine authority. The Code of Hammurabi, one of the earliest written legal codes, established rules for commerce, property, and social behavior. Although some of its punishments seem harsh by modern standards, the code represented an important step toward the rule of law. The legacy of Mesopotamian civilization can be seen in many aspects of modern culture, from legal systems to mathematical concepts.
