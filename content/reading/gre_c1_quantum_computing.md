Quantum computing represents a fundamental departure from classical computation, exploiting the principles of quantum mechanics — superposition, entanglement, and interference — to process information in ways that are theoretically impossible for classical machines. While a classical bit exists in one of two states, zero or one, a quantum bit, or qubit, can exist in a superposition of both states simultaneously, enabling exponential parallelism in certain computational tasks.

The theoretical foundations were laid by Richard Feynman and David Deutsch in the 1980s, who recognized that simulating quantum systems on classical computers was computationally intractable, but that a quantum computer could perform such simulations efficiently. Peter Shor's 1994 algorithm demonstrated that a sufficiently powerful quantum computer could factor large integers in polynomial time, threatening the security of RSA encryption, which underpins much of modern digital infrastructure.

Current quantum hardware faces formidable engineering challenges. Qubits are extraordinarily fragile — they decohere rapidly when exposed to environmental noise, a phenomenon known as quantum decoherence. Maintaining coherence requires cooling systems to temperatures near absolute zero, approximately 15 millikelvin, colder than outer space. Error rates in current systems remain high, necessitating quantum error correction schemes that require hundreds of physical qubits to encode a single logical qubit.

Despite these obstacles, progress has been remarkable. In 2019, Google claimed quantum supremacy, asserting that its 53-qubit Sycamore processor completed a specific calculation in 200 seconds that would take a classical supercomputer approximately 10,000 years. IBM, IonQ, and numerous startups are pursuing competing architectures, including superconducting qubits, trapped ions, and photonic systems.

The near-term applications most likely to yield practical advantage include drug discovery, materials science, optimization problems in logistics and finance, and cryptography. However, the timeline for fault-tolerant, general-purpose quantum computing capable of breaking current encryption standards remains uncertain, with estimates ranging from one to three decades.
