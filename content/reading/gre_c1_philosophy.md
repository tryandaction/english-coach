The philosophy of mind concerns itself with the nature of mental phenomena and their relationship to the physical world. Central to this field is the mind-body problem: how do subjective experiences — the redness of red, the pain of a headache — arise from purely physical processes in the brain? This question has occupied philosophers for centuries and remains unresolved despite significant advances in neuroscience.

Dualism, most famously articulated by René Descartes, holds that mind and body are fundamentally distinct substances. The mind, on this view, is non-physical and cannot be fully explained by the laws governing matter. Critics of dualism, however, point to the difficulty of explaining how a non-physical mind could causally interact with a physical body — a problem Descartes himself struggled to resolve.

Physicalism, the dominant position in contemporary philosophy of mind, asserts that mental states are ultimately physical states of the brain. Functionalism, a variant of physicalism, defines mental states by their functional roles — their causal relationships to sensory inputs, behavioral outputs, and other mental states — rather than by their physical composition. This view allows for the possibility of multiple realizability: the same mental state could in principle be instantiated in different physical substrates, including silicon-based systems.

The "hard problem" of consciousness, a term coined by philosopher David Chalmers, highlights what many see as the explanatory gap between physical descriptions of the brain and the subjective quality of experience. Even a complete neuroscientific account of how the brain processes information, Chalmers argues, would leave unexplained why there is something it is like to be a conscious creature at all.
