Artificial intelligence, or AI, refers to the ability of computer systems to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence, such as recognizing speech, making decisions, and translating languages. Over the past decade, AI has advanced rapidly, driven largely by a technique called machine learning, in which systems learn from large amounts of data rather than being explicitly programmed with rules.

Deep learning, a subset of machine learning, uses artificial neural networks inspired by the structure of the human brain. These networks consist of many layers of interconnected nodes that process information in increasingly abstract ways. Deep learning has achieved remarkable results in image recognition, natural language processing, and game playing, sometimes surpassing human performance.

Despite these achievements, current AI systems have significant limitations. They excel at narrow tasks for which they have been trained but struggle to generalize knowledge to new situations in the way humans can. They also lack common sense reasoning and can produce confident but incorrect outputs — a problem known as "hallucination" in language models.

The rapid development of AI raises important ethical questions. Concerns include the potential displacement of workers by automation, the use of AI in surveillance and decision-making systems that may perpetuate bias, and the long-term risks of increasingly powerful AI systems. Researchers and policymakers are working to develop frameworks for responsible AI development that maximize benefits while minimizing harms. The decisions made in the coming years about how to govern AI will have lasting consequences for society.
