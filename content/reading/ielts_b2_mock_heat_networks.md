When city governments discuss decarbonisation, attention often focuses on electricity: solar panels, wind farms, and electric vehicles. Yet in many climates, a large share of urban emissions still comes from heating buildings and providing hot water. This is why planners have renewed interest in district heating, sometimes called heat networks. Instead of each building using its own boiler, a heat network transports hot water or steam through insulated pipes from a central source to multiple homes, offices, schools, and hospitals. Supporters argue that such systems can become an important part of low-carbon urban infrastructure if they are modernised rather than treated as relics of an earlier industrial era.

Historically, district heating developed for practical reasons rather than climate policy. In dense nineteenth-century cities, centralising heat production could reduce local smoke, improve fuel efficiency, and simplify maintenance. Later systems connected entire neighbourhoods to combined heat and power plants that produced electricity and useful heat at the same time. In countries such as Denmark, Sweden, and parts of Eastern Europe, district heating became normal in many urban areas. However, in other places, cheap natural gas and the spread of individual boilers slowed network development, leaving many cities dependent on millions of separate heating units that are costly to replace one by one.

One reason heat networks are receiving new attention is that they can use energy sources that individual buildings usually cannot capture. Waste heat from data centres, metro tunnels, sewage systems, food-processing plants, and industrial facilities is often available at temperatures too low for traditional steam systems but suitable for newer low-temperature networks. Large electric heat pumps can upgrade this waste heat and feed it into the system. Geothermal energy, biomass, solar thermal fields, and thermal storage tanks can also be connected. As a result, the newest networks are not tied to one fuel source. Their value lies partly in flexibility: they allow cities to combine and switch among multiple heat inputs as technology and prices change.

Modern network design is also different from older high-temperature systems. Lower water temperatures reduce heat loss in pipes and make it easier to integrate buildings with better insulation and more efficient internal heating equipment. In some projects, planners retrofit buildings and network infrastructure together. Better windows, improved insulation, and smarter controls reduce the amount of heat required, which means pipes and pumps can be smaller and cheaper over time. This systems approach matters because a network is only as efficient as the buildings connected to it. If heat demand remains unnecessarily high, even a low-carbon source may be used inefficiently.

Despite the technical promise, implementation is not simple. Heat networks require long-term planning, road excavation, coordination among utilities, and substantial upfront capital. The benefits also emerge over time, while construction disruption is immediate and highly visible. Residents may tolerate a new bus lane or cycle path more easily than months of trenching in front of homes and shops. In liberalised energy markets, another challenge is governance: private investors want predictable returns, while public authorities are concerned with affordability and resilience. Cities therefore need regulatory frameworks that clarify ownership, consumer protection, and the rules for connecting new buildings or retrofitting existing districts.

Pricing is especially sensitive. If wealthier households can afford to keep individual heating systems while lower-income residents are effectively locked into a network, political support may weaken. On the other hand, if tariffs are poorly designed, operators may struggle to recover investment costs and maintain infrastructure quality. Some cities try to solve this by combining public ownership of the network with independent regulation of tariffs. Others subsidise the initial build-out in order to reduce long-term prices. The larger lesson is that heat networks are not merely engineering projects; they are governance projects that force cities to decide how energy costs and climate benefits should be shared.

Climate change strengthens the case for rethinking urban heat systems, but it also complicates planning. Some regions are likely to need less heating in winter and more cooling in summer. Engineers are therefore experimenting with thermal networks that can move energy in multiple directions and support both heating and cooling. The broader significance is that cities increasingly need infrastructure that can adapt over decades rather than serve one fixed pattern of demand. Heat networks fit this logic when they are designed as platforms capable of integrating new energy sources, storage technologies, and building standards as conditions change.

For that reason, many analysts argue that the debate should move beyond the question of whether district heating is old-fashioned. In reality, the issue is whether cities can build urban energy systems that are coordinated, flexible, and fair. Heat networks will not suit every settlement, especially low-density suburbs where pipe installation is expensive. But in dense districts with large institutional buildings and recoverable waste heat, they offer one of the few ways to decarbonise urban heating at scale. Their future depends less on whether the engineering is possible than on whether city governments can organise finance, regulation, and public trust well enough to make the transition durable.
