Natural selection, the mechanism at the heart of evolutionary theory, operates through the differential reproductive success of individuals within a population. Organisms that possess heritable traits conferring advantages in survival or reproduction leave more offspring, gradually shifting the composition of the population over successive generations. Charles Darwin's insight was to recognize that this simple process, operating over vast timescales, could account for the extraordinary diversity and complexity of life on Earth.

A persistent misconception is that evolution proceeds toward a predetermined goal or that organisms evolve traits "in order to" survive. In reality, natural selection is entirely blind to future needs; it acts only on variation that exists in the present population. Traits that happen to be advantageous in current environmental conditions become more prevalent, while disadvantageous traits are weeded out. This contingent, undirected character of evolution distinguishes it sharply from design-based explanations of biological complexity.

The modern evolutionary synthesis integrates Darwinian natural selection with Mendelian genetics and molecular biology. Mutations — random changes in DNA sequences — provide the raw material for evolution, while selection, genetic drift, gene flow, and sexual selection shape how genetic variation is distributed across populations. Horizontal gene transfer, once thought to be rare, has been found to play a significant role in the evolution of bacteria and other microorganisms, complicating the traditional tree-of-life model.

Evolutionary theory has profound implications beyond biology. It informs our understanding of antibiotic resistance, the origins of human behavior, and the conservation of biodiversity. The recognition that all living organisms share common ancestry represents one of the most unifying insights in the history of science.
