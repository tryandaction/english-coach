Traditional economics assumed that people make rational decisions based on complete information and self-interest. Behavioral economics challenges this assumption by incorporating insights from psychology to explain why people consistently make decisions that deviate from rational models.

One of the most influential concepts is loss aversion, developed by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky. Their research showed that people feel the pain of a loss approximately twice as strongly as the pleasure of an equivalent gain. This asymmetry explains many puzzling behaviors: why investors hold losing stocks too long, why people pay for insurance they rarely need, and why negotiators focus more on avoiding concessions than on achieving gains.

The concept of choice architecture — how options are presented — has profound implications for policy. Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein demonstrated that default options have enormous power. When employees are automatically enrolled in pension plans and must actively opt out, participation rates rise dramatically compared to systems where enrollment requires active opt-in. This insight has been used to increase retirement savings rates in the United Kingdom and the United States without restricting individual freedom.

Cognitive biases also affect consumer behavior. The anchoring effect shows that people rely heavily on the first piece of information they encounter. A product initially priced at £200 and reduced to £120 feels like a bargain, even if £120 is still above market value. Retailers exploit this systematically through artificial "original prices."

The practical applications of behavioral economics extend to public health, energy conservation, tax compliance, and financial regulation. Governments worldwide have established "nudge units" to apply these insights to policy design, recognizing that small changes in how choices are presented can produce large changes in behavior without coercion or financial incentives.
