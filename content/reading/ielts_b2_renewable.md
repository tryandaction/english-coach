Renewable energy comes from natural sources that are constantly replenished, such as sunlight, wind, rain, and geothermal heat. Unlike fossil fuels, which release carbon dioxide when burned, renewable energy sources produce little or no greenhouse gas emissions. As concerns about climate change grow, many countries are accelerating their transition to renewable energy.

Solar power has become one of the fastest-growing energy sources in the world. Photovoltaic panels convert sunlight directly into electricity and can be installed on rooftops, in fields, or even on water surfaces. The cost of solar panels has fallen dramatically over the past decade, making solar energy competitive with traditional power sources in many regions.

Wind energy is another rapidly expanding sector. Wind turbines capture the kinetic energy of moving air and convert it into electricity. Offshore wind farms, located in the sea where winds are stronger and more consistent, are becoming increasingly common in Europe and Asia. However, wind energy has limitations — it only generates power when the wind is blowing, which creates challenges for grid stability.

Energy storage technology is crucial for overcoming the intermittent nature of solar and wind power. Batteries can store excess energy generated during peak production periods and release it when demand is high. Advances in battery technology, particularly lithium-ion batteries, are making storage solutions more affordable and efficient. The combination of renewable generation and improved storage is seen as key to building a reliable, low-carbon energy system.
