Sleep is one of the most important activities for human health, yet it is often the first thing people sacrifice when life gets busy. Scientists now understand that sleep is not simply a period of rest — it is an active process during which the brain and body perform essential maintenance and repair.

During sleep, the brain consolidates memories, transferring information from short-term to long-term storage. This is why students who sleep well after studying tend to remember more than those who stay up all night. The brain also clears waste products during sleep, including proteins associated with Alzheimer's disease, through a system called the glymphatic system.

The body uses sleep to repair tissues, produce hormones, and strengthen the immune system. Growth hormone, which is essential for muscle repair and development, is released primarily during deep sleep. People who consistently sleep less than seven hours per night have higher rates of obesity, diabetes, heart disease, and depression.

Sleep deprivation affects cognitive performance in ways that people often underestimate. After 17 to 19 hours without sleep, performance on cognitive tests is equivalent to having a blood alcohol level of 0.05 percent. After 24 hours, it equals 0.10 percent — above the legal driving limit in most countries.

Most adults need between seven and nine hours of sleep per night. Teenagers need eight to ten hours because their brains are still developing. Establishing a consistent sleep schedule, avoiding screens before bed, and keeping the bedroom cool and dark are among the most effective strategies for improving sleep quality.
