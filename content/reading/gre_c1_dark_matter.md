One of the most profound mysteries in modern physics is the nature of dark matter — a hypothetical form of matter that does not interact with the electromagnetic force and therefore cannot be directly observed, yet whose gravitational effects are essential to explaining the structure and dynamics of the universe.

The evidence for dark matter is compelling and comes from multiple independent lines of observation. In the 1970s, astronomer Vera Rubin measured the rotation curves of spiral galaxies and found that stars at the outer edges rotate at roughly the same speed as those near the center — a result inconsistent with the distribution of visible matter. The only explanation consistent with Newtonian gravity was the presence of a massive, invisible halo of matter surrounding each galaxy.

Gravitational lensing provides further evidence. Massive objects bend light according to general relativity, and observations of galaxy clusters show far more lensing than can be accounted for by visible matter alone. The Bullet Cluster, formed by the collision of two galaxy clusters, is particularly striking: the hot gas — the dominant visible component — was slowed by electromagnetic interactions during the collision, while the dark matter halos passed through each other unimpeded, creating a spatial separation between visible and dark matter that can be directly mapped.

Despite decades of searching, the particle nature of dark matter remains unknown. The leading candidates include weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs), axions, and sterile neutrinos. Direct detection experiments buried deep underground to shield against cosmic ray interference have so far yielded null results for WIMPs, placing increasingly stringent constraints on their properties.

Some physicists have proposed alternative theories, such as Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND), which adjusts the laws of gravity rather than invoking new particles. However, MOND struggles to explain observations like the Bullet Cluster that dark matter handles naturally. The resolution of this mystery will likely require either the detection of a dark matter particle or a fundamental revision of our understanding of gravity.
